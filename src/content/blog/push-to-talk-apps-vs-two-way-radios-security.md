## The Question Every Guard Company Eventually Asks

Sooner or later, every owner prices out replacement radios, looks at the phones already in every guard's pocket, and asks whether a push to talk app can retire the security radios entirely. The honest answer from anyone who has run both: it depends on your sites, and the best-run operations increasingly land on a hybrid. This is the comparison we wish someone had handed us — trade-off by trade-off, without the vendor gloss from either side.

## What Each One Actually Is

**Two-way radios** (analog or digital, licensed or license-free) transmit directly between handsets or through a repeater. No internet involved. Range is physics: power, antennas, terrain, and building construction.

**Push-to-talk (PTT) apps** run on smartphones over LTE/5G or Wi-Fi. Coverage is the carrier's network, not your repeater. The "radio" is software — channels, groups, and history live on a server.

## Head-to-Head: Push to Talk App vs. Security Radios

| Factor | Two-way radios | PTT app on smartphones |
|---|---|---|
| Coverage | Site-limited; excellent within range, dead beyond it | Anywhere with cellular/Wi-Fi — cross-city, cross-state |
| Dead zones | Basements, stairwells, metal buildings without repeaters | Cellular dead spots; often better indoors on Wi-Fi |
| Latency | Effectively instant | Slight connect delay; noticeable but workable on good networks |
| Hardware cost | Dedicated handsets, chargers, spare batteries, repeater if needed | Phones guards often already carry |
| Durability | Purpose-built; survives drops and rain | Depends on the phone and case |
| Battery through a 12-hour shift | Strong; swap batteries in seconds | Phones need charging discipline and battery policy |
| Simplicity under stress | One knob, one button — nothing to unlock | Must be as close to one-press as the app allows |
| Recording & history | Rare without extra infrastructure | Native: voice history, timestamps, who-said-what |
| Beyond voice | Voice only | Location, panic alerts, messaging, photos on the same device |
| Failure mode | Repeater/handset failure — you fix it | Carrier outage — you wait |

## Where Radios Still Win

- **Single compact sites with hostile RF conditions you've already solved.** If you've invested in a repeater that blankets a high-rise or plant, that infrastructure keeps working during a carrier outage and costs nothing more per month.
- **Instant, guaranteed voice.** For a tight tactical team working one building, the zero-setup immediacy of a radio channel is still the gold standard.
- **Environments where phones are prohibited** — some industrial, correctional, or client-policy contexts simply bar smartphones on post.
- **Battery brutality.** A guard can pocket a spare radio battery. Phone charging on a 12-hour outdoor post takes planning.

## Where PTT Apps Win

- **Geography.** The moment your operation spans multiple sites — or a mobile patrol crosses town — radios stop being one network. A PTT channel doesn't care whether the supervisor is at the site, the office, or another city. Dispatch talks to every post in the company from one screen.
- **One device, whole workflow.** The same phone that carries the PTT channel carries clock-in, checkpoint scans, incident reports with photos, and the panic button. - **Accountability.** App-based PTT leaves a record: transmission history, timestamps, participants. When a client asks "was the alarm called in promptly?", you answer from the log, not from memory. Radios give you silence.
- **Cost structure for growing companies.** No repeater engineering per new site. Winning a contract across town doesn't trigger an infrastructure project — the channel already covers it.
- **Onboarding speed.** A new guard installs an app and joins the channel in minutes.

## The Honest Weak Points of App PTT

Take these seriously before you cut over:

- **Network dependence.** No signal, no voice. Survey each site's cellular and Wi-Fi reality before committing, especially basements and rural posts.
- **Phone policy questions.** Company phones or BYOD? Who pays the data? What happens when a guard's personal phone dies mid-shift? Write the policy first.
- **Press-to-talk ergonomics.** If reaching the PTT button takes three taps, guards won't use it under pressure. Insist on lock-screen or one-touch access.

## The Hybrid Answer Most Operations Land On

In practice, the strongest setups mix both:

- **App PTT as the company-wide backbone** — every guard, supervisor, and dispatcher on organized channels, with history and location context.
- **Radios retained at specific sites** where existing repeater coverage, client policy, or RF conditions justify them — often bridged into the app network so radio traffic and app traffic share a channel.

That gives you radio resilience where it's paid for and app reach everywhere else, with dispatch hearing everything in one place. It also stages your migration: new sites launch app-only, legacy radio sites keep working, and you retire hardware on your schedule instead of a forklift cutover. ## How to Decide

1. Map your sites: square footage, construction, cellular signal, existing radio assets.
2. Count devices and true costs: handsets, batteries, repeater maintenance vs. phones, plans, app licensing.
3. Pilot the app at one multi-guard site for 30 days alongside existing radios.
4. Measure what changed: missed calls, response coordination, and what the voice history let you prove to a client.

## The Bottom Line

Radios are superb site infrastructure; PTT apps are company-wide communication plus a full operations toolkit on the same device. If you run one building with a good repeater, keep it. If you run a growing multi-site operation, the app backbone wins — and a bridge keeps your radio investment in the conversation.
