## Buddy punching: the payroll leak security companies pretend not to have

Every owner wants to prevent buddy punching security operations quietly bleed money through: one guard clocks in for another who's running late, still asleep, or not coming at all. Sometimes it's a paper sign-in sheet filled out generously. Sometimes it's a shared PIN at a time clock. Sometimes it's just a text to dispatch — "Mike's on post" — that nobody verifies. Either way, you're paying wages for hours nobody worked, and worse: you're billing a client for a post that sat empty.

That second part is what makes buddy punching different in security than in a warehouse or a restaurant. An empty post isn't just payroll shrinkage — it's contract breach, liability exposure if an incident happens during the gap, and the fastest way to lose an account if the client finds out before you do.

## Why traditional time tracking fails for guards

Guard work breaks every assumption normal time clocks were built on:

- **No central location.** Guards report directly to client sites, so there's no wall clock at HQ to punch. Paper sheets live at the post, unsupervised.
- **No manager present.** At most posts, especially overnight, nobody with authority witnesses the start of shift.
- **Phone-based check-ins are unverifiable.** "I'm on site" over the phone or WhatsApp proves the guard is awake, not where they are.
- **Shared credentials.** PIN pads and punch cards authenticate a code or a card — not a person. Anyone with the code can punch anyone in.

If your attendance system can't answer "which person, at which location, at what time" with evidence, buddy punching isn't a risk — it's a certainty. The only question is how much of it you're funding.

## The fix: verify person + place + time on every punch

Modern guard attendance closes all three gaps at once with a mobile clock-in from the guard's own phone:

| Verification | How it works | What it kills |
|---|---|---|
| Place | GPS check confirms the punch happens at the post, inside a geofence | Clocking in from home or the bus |
| Person | Photo capture or biometric check on the guard's own device at punch | Punching for a coworker |
| Time | Server-side timestamp, not device clock | Backdating and "adjusted" sheets |

The combination matters. GPS alone stops remote punching but not on-site swaps (one guard covering while carrying two phones is rare but not impossible). Adding a punch-time photo or a device tied to the individual guard makes impersonation impractical. And because the record is server-timestamped and immutable, the Friday ritual of "correcting" the timesheet disappears.

## The overlooked half: catching the empty post in real time

Preventing fraudulent punches is only half the win. The other half is what verified attendance makes possible: a live coverage board. When every clock-in is real, dispatch can trust a simple screen that shows every post — covered, late, or open — right now.

That changes your failure mode completely:

- **Before:** guard no-shows, buddy covers the punch, client discovers the empty post, you find out from an angry email.
- **After:** no verified punch by 5–10 minutes past start triggers an alert; dispatch calls the guard, activates the standby, and notifies the client proactively if needed.

The company that calls its client to say "your relief officer was late, coverage was restored at 11:20, here's the record" keeps the contract. The company that gets caught keeps a lawyer.

## Rolling it out without a mutiny

Guards aren't the enemy here — most punch honestly and end up resenting the few who don't. But a sloppy rollout can make honest guards feel accused. What works:

1. **Frame it as protection, and mean it.** Verified attendance is the guard's proof they showed up on time when a client claims otherwise, and their guarantee of accurate pay including overtime. Both happen constantly.
2. **Announce it; don't ambush.** Give a start date, run a one-week grace period for mistakes, and train everyone on the app in five minutes (that's genuinely all a clock-in takes).
3. **Solve the edge cases up front.** Guard without a smartphone? Basement post with no GPS fix? Decide the fallback procedure (supervisor verification, site device) before day one so exceptions don't become excuses.
4. **Enforce evenly.** The first time a supervisor's friend gets a pass on a fake punch, the whole system loses credibility. Progressive discipline, applied to everyone, written down.
5. **Pay the flip side promptly.** Verified time cuts both ways — when the record shows 20 minutes of holdover because relief was late, pay it without a fight. Guards adopt systems that treat them fairly.
