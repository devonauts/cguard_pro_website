## The Math Behind 24 Hour Security Post Scheduling

A 24/7 post looks simple on the contract — "one officer, around the clock" — and then destroys your margins in execution. Getting 24 hour security post scheduling right comes down to accepting one number up front: a single continuous post consumes 168 hours per week, and no arrangement of full-time guards divides into that cleanly without a relief plan. 168 hours ÷ 40-hour weeks = 4.2 guards. Not 4. That fraction — the relief factor — is where coverage gaps, surprise overtime, and burned-out guards come from when owners try to staff the post with exactly four people.

Plan for more than the raw math, too. Guards take vacation, get sick, quit, and attend training. Whatever headcount the arithmetic gives you, the operating reality demands a bench behind it.

## Pick a Shift Pattern Deliberately

There is no universally correct pattern; there are trade-offs you should choose with open eyes.

| Pattern | Structure | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3 × 8-hour | Day / evening / night | Less fatigue per shift; easier to fill a single call-off | Two handovers a day; more guards to recruit |
| 2 × 12-hour | Day / night | One handover; guards like the compressed week | Fatigue in hours 10–12; a call-off leaves a 12-hour hole |
| 4-on / 4-off (12s) | Rotating teams | Predictable for guards; simple to communicate | Requires four teams; odd payroll weeks |
| Fixed vs. rotating shifts | Either 8s or 12s | Fixed shifts protect sleep; rotating spreads night burden | Rotating hurts retention if the rotation is fast |

Two rules from hard experience:

- **Overnight is where contracts die.** Whatever pattern you pick, put your steadiest people on nights and supervise them like it matters — because it does.
- **Fixed nights beat rotating nights for most small companies.** Some guards genuinely prefer nights. Find them, pay the differential, keep them.

## The Relief Guard Is a Position, Not an Afterthought

The single biggest structural fix for gap-free coverage is treating relief as a scheduled role. A dedicated relief guard (or pool) exists to absorb the days off your pattern creates. Build it explicitly:

- Map every recurring off-day the pattern generates across the week.
- Assign relief guards to those slots on the published schedule — by name, in advance.
- Cross-train relief guards on every post they cover, including site-specific post orders. A relief guard who has never seen the site is a warm body, not coverage.

If your relief plan is "the supervisor will find someone," you don't have a relief plan. You have a future 3 a.m. phone call.

## Killing Coverage Gaps Operationally

The schedule on paper and the post at 2 a.m. are different worlds. Close the gap between them:

- **Verified clock-in at the post, not from the parking lot.** GPS-anchored clock-in through a mobile app tells dispatch the post is actually manned. CGuardPro's [guard app](/en/app-movil) flags a late or missing clock-in within minutes, which converts "we found out from the client" into "we dispatched relief before the client noticed."
- **No-show protocol with a clock on it.** Define exactly what happens at +5, +15, and +30 minutes past shift start: call the guard, call the standby, dispatch a supervisor. Write it down; drill it.
- **Overlap the handover.** A 15-minute paid overlap for pass-down beats a cold start every time. The outgoing guard hands over open items; the incoming guard signs for them. Gaps hide in handovers as often as in no-shows.
- **One source of truth for the schedule.** If the schedule lives in a spreadsheet, a group chat, and a whiteboard simultaneously, guards will work from the stale copy. Publish once, update in one place, notify automatically.

## Controlling Overtime Without Creating Holes

Overtime on a 24-hour post is usually a symptom, not a cause. Chase the sources:

- **Understaffing the relief factor** — the fraction you ignored becomes mandatory OT.
- **Uneven distribution** — the same two reliable guards absorb every call-off until they burn out and quit, which makes the problem worse. Turnover and overtime feed each other; our piece on [reducing guard turnover](/en/blog/reduce-security-guard-turnover-strategies) goes deeper.
- **Invisible creep** — early clock-ins and late clock-outs of 10–20 minutes a shift, unnoticed until payroll. Digital time capture makes it visible weekly instead of quarterly.

Set an overtime review cadence — weekly, per post — and treat any post that consistently runs OT as a staffing-model problem to redesign, not a payroll line to tolerate.

## Build the Schedule Backwards From Failure

The strongest scheduling habit is pre-mortem thinking: assume the call-off, then check whether the schedule already answers it.

1. For every shift on the schedule, name the first backup.
2. For every backup, confirm they're trained on that post and within legal/fatigue limits.
3. For the worst case — two call-offs on a holiday night — know which supervisor covers and which client gets a proactive call.

If any of those three questions takes more than a minute to answer, your schedule is fragile. Software should answer them instantly: who's qualified, who's available, who's approaching overtime, and one tap to offer the shift.

## The Bottom Line

Gap-free 24/7 coverage isn't heroics — it's arithmetic plus discipline. Staff to the real relief factor, choose a shift pattern for your workforce rather than your spreadsheet, make relief a named position, verify presence digitally, and rehearse the no-show protocol before you need it. Do that and "the post was never empty" becomes something you can prove to a client, not just promise.
